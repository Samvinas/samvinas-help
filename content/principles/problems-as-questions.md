# Framing problems as questions

Statements close; questions open. The same difficulty, framed as a verdict —
*"it's too expensive"* — ends a conversation, while framed as a question —
*"how might we bring the cost down?"* — starts one. Turning problems into
open, invitational questions is a small habit of language with outsized
effects on what a group can do.

## The stem: "How might we…?"

Each word is doing work:

- **How** presumes there's a way — the question asks for routes, not
  permission.
- **might** lowers the stakes — answers are candidates, not commitments, so
  wilder ones can surface.
- **we** makes it shared — the problem stops belonging to whoever raised it.

"How might we…?" also has siblings worth rotating through when a frame feels
stiff: *"How to…?"*, *"In what ways might we…?"*, and — for deliberately
widening a hunt — *"What might be all the…?"*.

## Before the problem: the wish

Question stems have an upstream relative. Early in
[Clarify](/principles/four-stages.html), before anything is defined, the
right frame isn't a question but a **wish**: *"I wish…"*, *"It would be
great if…"*. A wish does for goals what "How might we…?" does for problems —
it states an appetite without committing to a solution, so many can be
voiced before one is chosen. Once a wish is picked, the question stems take
over.

## Getting the altitude right

A question can be framed too broad or too narrow. *"How might we improve the
company?"* gives a group nothing to grip; *"How might we reword the third
sentence of the welcome email?"* has already decided the answer. Test a
frame by asking **"why?"** (to go broader) and **"what's stopping us?"** (to
go narrower) until the question feels both open and concrete — the
[Five Whys](/facilitator/five-whys.html) tool walks this ladder deliberately.

## Where Samvinas leans on this

- A good brainstorm prompt *is* a well-framed question — write "How might
  we…?" rather than a topic heading.
- In [PPCO](/facilitator/ppco.html) and its relatives, concerns are phrased
  as questions precisely so they can be *overcome* rather than merely
  recorded.

Framing and [deferring judgement](/principles/deferring-judgement.html) are
partners: one keeps the door open, the other keeps ideas walking through it.
